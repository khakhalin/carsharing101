# 00. Introduction

# Foreword

Let's be honest, as I am writing this text in 2025, the public image of carsharing is not particularly stellar[^1]. During the era of cheap money of the 2010s, the field was plagued by provider over-saturation. Some markets that could only support 1-2 competing networks had 4-5 providers engaged in price wars[^2]. This period was followed by a series of unproductive mergers and acquisitions[^weshare][^f2m_purchase][^f2m_sale]), chaotic market exits and expansions[^brussels_plus][^brussels_plus2][^brussels_minus], and even legal scandals[^miles]. Combined with changes in political climate, where at least in some cities urbanization and quality of life got threatened by reactionary governments[^ontario][^berlin_cdu][^trump], it created a narrative of distrust towards carsharing as a business model. This unfortunate history made many people believe that carsharing simply cannot be profitable; that it is somehow a dead-end, akin to other unsustainable fads of the 2010s, like daily deal sites or on-demand laundry.

The main reason I started working on this manual is that, as a data scientist, and an advocate for car-minimalistic future, I don't think that this perception is true! I believe that it is absolutely possible to build a profitable free-floating carsharing business, and that all the necessary parts for this to happen already do exist on the market, just not necessarily within a single company. A good car-sharing company should have good management, good cars, good tech stack, and manage its operations wisely. Now that I no longer work in carsharing, I cannot help with the first three problems, but I can still help with the last, by publishing this work, and hopefully helping all competing companies at once to improve their operations. At worst, you can use these texts to onboard new team members. At best, you can learn something from them, and boost your profitability here and there. Or one can also try to use this collection of models as a backbone for a practical introductory course in transportation data science.

The writing here alternates between basic common sense considerations, mathematical models, simulation outputs, and **practical business advice** that stem from these models. I will use GitHub-specific Markdown to drive these practical points down. For example, you'll see business tips in the text, shown liks this:

At the risk of stating the obvious, I should probably also mention what this work is NOT: in what aspects of carsharing it won't help you. It won't discuss systems engineering: how to actually version and deliver the models, how to write microservices, and how to put the flexible pricing system in production. It won't discuss how to collect and store the data, what levels of data aggregation are ideal for a carsharing business, and how to satisfy GDPR. It won't deeply discuss the matters of operations and security, even though of course these can also be automated and optimize. It won't discuss neither the backend nor the design of the app (even though A/B testing is often managed by the data science team), and it won't discuss marketing (even though in real life marketing and customer management should also be supported by the data science team). And it will mention the politics of carsharing, the negotiations with the cities, and the public-facing communications, only once or twice, in passing (even though the data science team often has a direct input to these communications and strategic negotiations). This work presents the conceptual models, the math, and the modeling of this math, but all the actual flesh and blood of a data-driven organization, sadly, is not covered here.

# The structure of this work

This document / repository consists of the following texts:

* **0. Introduction** - the text you're reading right now!
* **1. Natural behavior of cars** - a series of models that show what will happen with a car-sharing business if you let the cars be rented, and don't try to actively manage them. In some ways, it is the most important chapter in the whole thing, as it describes the cardinal problem of car-sharing; the problem that the following three chapters are then trying to fight.
* **2. Relocations** - a study for how to best move cars within the operating area, to achieve the best performance. This chapter also, oddly, contains a section on fleet planning (on deciding how many cars to keep in the city).
* **4. Operating area** - this chapter describes how to optimally expand and constrict the dynamic area, to achieve profitability while also improving offerings on the market.
* **3. Pricing** - while the topic of pricing in carsharing is rich and complicated, here I touch only on some minor aspects of it, leaving the most interesting topics, such as dynamic pricing, reinforcement learning, and policy optimization, beyond the scope.
* **5. Conclusions** - a few random thoughts that didn't make it to any other chapter.
* **6. Appendix** - a justification for some of the numerical assumptions used in this work.

# Core terms and concepts

Before we dive into the depth of carsharing-related data science, **we need to agree on common terminology**. To be honest, I am not quite sure how standard this terminology is, and whether a "standard terminology" even exists, as far as carsharing is concerned, but there are a few terms that are consistently used throughout this document, so it would only be prudent to discuss them upfront.

We abstract our **cars** as points, standing on a **map**, waiting to be **rented**. Once rented, cars will disappear from the map for some time, as they are driven, and then re-appear in a new place, as they are parked. We won't concern ourselves with the route that rented cars take through the map: these routes are the private business of our customers, we don't track it, we are not trying to optimize it: for all practical purposes rented cars just disappear from the map for a while, to later resurface in a different location.

In simulations, we will assume the rental of a car to happen instantaneously: one moment the car is standing there, open for business, the next moment it is rented. In real life everything is of course a bit more complicated: for most, if not all, carsharing services a car can be "booked" as the customer is walking towards it, either for free, or for a small fee, so it does not transition from an "available" to a "rented" state instantaneously. We will be talking about these phenomena every now and then, while discussing customer behavior, demand, and car placement, but we won't be modeling them explicitly! We also won't be modeling broken cars, cars that are waiting for a service, and cars that need to be recharged. All of these states are out of scope of this work.

As a customer finishes a rental, the car becomes available again, but finishing a rental is possible only when a car is positioned properly: if it is standing in a good, reasonable place, where it can keep standing, waiting for another customer. In simpler simulations we will assume that our business operates on a small set of "**stations**": mobility hubs, or dedicated parking spots (we'll usually model 10 of them). Each of these parking spots will be assumed to be a point in space (lacking any internal structure), and have an infinite capacity. When discussing continuous cities, we will be talking about "**zones**": parts of the city, that can be either large or small, and that we use to track the cars, and plan their movements. In most discussions, words "stations" and "zones" can be used interchangeably, but I tried to stick to "stations" for discrete cases, and to "zones" for continuous cases. The total of all stations and zones form an **Operating Area**: the part of the city in which cars can be parked, once the rental is over, and from which, by extension, they can be rented again. We will even have a whole chapter (Chapter 3) about how to optimize the Operating Area for a given city.

We will be often trying to calculate different kinds of **KPI**s (Key Performance Indicators, which is a business slang for loss functions and objective functions) at the level of individual zones and stations, such as, for example, the total number of rentals and arrivals, the average number of cars standing there at any moment of time, service and financial measures (see below), and idle times. The average idle time for a zone is just the average of all uninterrupted periods between the end of a previous rental, and the start of the next rentals, for cars that are parked in a zone.

Within a station or a zone, all cars will be assumed to be perfectly interchangeable. In practice, it is not true of course, and we'll talk a little bit about it in chapters 02 (Relocations) and 04 (Pricing): we may have a portfolio of smaller and larger cars, different models, cars of different age; cars that are cosmetically damaged, cars that are dirty from the outside, or not exceptionally clean inside, as their turn to be cleaned haven't arrived yet. We will ignore all of these aspects in this work, same as we ignore the operational model of cleaning and servicing cars. We will also assume that the cars are perfectly charged, and that the amount of charging they hold is not a factor for either customers, or our own decisions.

When talking about **demand** in a zone or at a station, we will imagine it as a "desire to rent a car" that customers at this zone or station randomly and spontaneously manifest every now and then. Mathematically, we will think of demand as of intensity of a point process happening in this zone, but we'll talk more about it in the very beginning Chapter 1, so don't get worried about it just yet. What matters is that every now and then a customer would suddenly come up with an idea to maybe rent a car of ours; they would open an imaginary app, look at an imaginary map, check the price of the car, the distance that they would have to walk to this car, and then decide to either rent it or not. Because of this imaginary process, sometimes I would refer to demand as "**app openings**". Still, in most simulations we will assume that the "true demand" is kinda known, or at least "exists" out there, as a platonic abstraction of the intensity of app opening events that we can actually observe. We won't model app openings explicitly, but just be aware that it's the mental model we may have in mind when discussing customer demand.

When discussing service, we will use a standard measure of **Demand Fulfilling Rate**, or **DFR**, defined as the share of "app opening events" in which a customer did discover a car nearby, and could then decide to either rent or not rent it. In real life customers may also be just playing with the app, or plan some future travels, or idly checking prices; they could also first physically discover a car parked on the street, and only then use an app to rent it; we will however ignore all of these scenarios. These are all valid finer points when a real business is trying to interpret spatial and temporal aspects of their app openings stream, especially in usual places, like airports, train stations, trains themselves, etc., but we will leave them out of scope of this work.

We now arrived at two last, but probably most important KPIs used in these texts: the concepts of **Contribution Margins**, labeled **CM1** and **CM2**[^cm1]. When discussing the financials of our imaginary carsharing business, we will use the following hierarchy of financial indicators:

* **Revenue** is the actual money received from customers: the price per unit of service (distance or time), multiplied by the price, and collected across all rentals. In real life there is often a difference between Gross Revenue and Net Revenue, as the Value-Added-Tax is getting inviolved, but we will ignore all of this. Our Revenue is always the Net Revenue.
* **CM1** (Contribution Margin 1) is defined as Revenue minus **variable costs**, or costs associated with the very process of delivering our service to the customer. For carsharing, we will include in CM1 all costs associated with either distance and time driven, or with the number of rentals, which would, for example, include the costs for:
    - Electric charge (formerly known as fuel), which you obviously spend as you drive
    - Cleaning, and planned servicing of cars. As mentioned, we won't be explicitly modeling any of these aspects of our business, but we'll still need to estimate their costs! We'll do it in Chapter 06 (the Appenidix) to make sure our financial numbers are roughy  realistic.
    - The human work involved in recharging of cars, when it happens outside of rentals.
    - The fixing of small damages and blemishes that cars accumulate, when they are not covered by insurance.
    - Customer support centers. This last point may sound a bit surprising, but if you think of it, customers usually need support when they are using the car, and run into one issue or another (which is probably rouhgly proportional to minutes driven), or if they have follow-ups after a problematic rental (which is proportional to the number of rentals). If we also consider that most customer support centers these days can be rented and scaled on demand, this cost is clearly belongs to the "variable costs" department.
* * **CM2** (Contribution Margin 2) is defined as CM1 minus **fixed costs**, or the costs that are directly associated with your product or your service (so _not_ the overhead costs!), but that you bear regardless of whether you managed to sell anything or not on any particular day. For most "normal" businesses these include, for example, the costs of holding the product-producing equipment, but in case of carsharing the equipment is _cars_, which means that carsharing CM2 includes the costs of:
    - Leasing or owning the cars (amortized over time). As shown in the appendix, this is the main part of the CM2 cost.
    - Leasing or owning (amortized) all the additional equipment that needs to go into cars to make them suitable for carsharing, such as the telemetry equipment, safety equipment, trackers, mobile data contracts etc.
    - Per-car insurance
    - Payments for permits to operate at any given city or region, which are often also proportional to the number of cars involved.
* **EBIT** (Earnings Before Interest and Taxes) cam be defined as CM2 minus overhead (all the administrative, marketing, and general operational expenses). A positive EBIT is one of the most sensible measures of a profitable business (even if technically in the books everything may be a bit more complicated). Except for one paragaraph in the Chapter 05 (Conclusion) we won't be concerned with EBIT and profitability here, but I wanted to include it in this list, to better show how CM1 and CM2 fall on the overall scale, from raw revenue to profitability of the company as a whole.

As you can see, all four terms (Revenue, CM1, CM2, and EBIT) are measured in units of money (in this work, in €). The Revenue is always positive. The CM1 is certainly positive on average, and also positive for the vast majority of individual rentals, as it is not normal for a company to offer services for a price that is lower than the cost of delivering these services[^berlin_parking], although occasional exceptions may happen. Once we come to CM2 however, the positivity of this margin is no longer a given! For a company as a whole to be profitable, it of course has to have a positive total CM2, and moreover, the CM2 should be positive enough to cover all the overhead expenses that will be included in EBIT. But at the same time, if a company is at a state of a price war, or is heavily expanding its services in a new region, or is trying to convince customers to switch from private car ownership to shared car usage, it may temporarily go with a near-zero or even mildly negative CM2. But either way, as you will see, the sign of CM2 will constitute the main drama and intrigue of pretty much all simulations presented in this work.

Now that we defined the contribution margins, let's briefly talk about the practical side of their use. Calculating CM2, assuming that CM1 is known, sounds quite doable, as most new costs CM2 is supposed to account for arrive in "large chunks": the price of a batch of cars, the price of extra equipment for these cars, a yearly permission to operate etc. In contract, the costs included in CM1 are comprised of thousands, if not millions, of tiny expenses. Is it really possible to track and book every incurred cost, every scratch, every customer call, in real-time, and also somehow link them to rentals that caused them? Is it possible, is it practical, and is it really necessary? If you think of this problem, the simplest practical solution would be to aggregate these costs by type, over a longer period of time (say, about a month), and only then allocate them to rentals. For "classical businesses", CM1 is supposed to be proportional to the amount of "good sold"; in the "carsharing universe" this will correspond to "minutes driven", as the most natural and easy to obtain proxy for "usage". In real life, a data scientist would probably want to build a fancier regression model, allocating different aspects of CM1 to different usage factors, arriving at something like a "fixed cost per rental + costs proportional to distance driven + costs proportional to time"), and then comparing CM1 and CM2 profitability of  different products within a portfolio (different car models, cities, usage packages etc.). But for the sake of this work, we will just assume that CM1 costs are proportional to the time driven.

When running the  models, we will try to stick to a list of three very simple and round numbers for CM1 and CM2: we will assume that a typical rental brings us about €10 in Revenue; that it also costs us about €5 in variable costs (bringing the CM1 profit per rental to €5), and that owning a car costs us about 20 €/day in CM2. Despite their suspicious roundness, these numbers are aligned with reality for 2025, and are carefully justified in Chapter 6 (the Appendix).

I think at this point we know enough, and are quite ready to start developing the models. Let's go!
